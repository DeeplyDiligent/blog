Let's Talk: Avoiding Monotonity

Jul 16, 2018

It happens to the best of us. Be it your favourite food, movie, game, job or hobby, we all get bored at some point. Why?

Let Me Tell You a Story

My parents are really hard working middle-class people. Day in day out they spend their time diligently climbing their ways up the corporate ladder, trying to get the biggest slice of the pie with the least amount of work. However, when doing this, they are exceptionally bored with their daily lives. They look for holidays and weekends as an escape from their otherwise dull workplaces.

Why It Never Works

Ever watched a horse on a race track? It was fast wasn’t it? Now let’s say you’re in the era where no cars existed. You’re a horse taxi service which takes riders from location A to B in the least amount of time possible, the smaller the time, the more happy riders, and the more money you can potentially earn. What will you try to do to boost your profits? Recruit the fastest horses you can possibly get of course! You might even try to genetically engineer horses to make them faster and stronger.

I hope you can see why this strategy never works. The corporate ladder is engineered for long days and sleepless nights. It is carved so that its deciples are unable to see the bigger picture.

Tread Your Own Path

How I avoid monotonity in the workplace:

Your values are the most crucial tool to solving the riddle of life. The priority that you give to each value will, over the long term, determine where you end up. Having good values is like learning the steps for solving a rubics cube. Just like there’s an incredibly minute chance that someone who has just picked up a properly scrambled rubics cube for the first time in their life will be able to solve it, there is also very little chance that that someone with inconsistent or vague values will be able to navigate life. Values must be prioritised, and we must ensure we are true to our values using a tactic I will introdouce next time called auto suggestion, first published in Napoleon Hill’s book “Think and Grow Rich”.
