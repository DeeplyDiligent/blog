When I was much younger and struggled to get my homework done on its due date, I would become terrified. We know that every single task is like a small mountain that we need to climb and that theres a drop at the end where the grass will be a lot greener.

Conquering Mount Everest

I‚Äôm sure you have a task with a high importance, a task that will make a crucial impact on your life, that is currently too big for you to do. Two possible issues are that you may have many different smaller tasks which are more urgent (have a closer due date) or you have no clue where to start the big task. This is your Mount Everest. Lets take you through conquering it

Getting to the bottom

Before we can start climbing we must push all other tasks aside, leaving just the mountain itself. This is through prioritisation. Grab a pencil and start batching all the smaller tasks, then complete them one after another with minimal time wasted. Any tasks that you can defer, do it!

Starting the climb

Whether you cant think of somewhere to begin, or cant muster the motivation to put pen to paper, I guarantee that if you:

  • Think of a tiny part of the task (read the assignment, write the first paragraph etc)
  • Allocate a set time that you think will be enough for it

You will find the task at least partially interesting and doable. That initial hump is the biggest. A rule in physics is that a system is much easier to keep going than it is to restart. I believe this principle applies nicely here as well. All the best!

Productivity Tools (Part 2)

Jul 20, 2018

You might be thinking to yourself right now, all these concentration development tactics are great and all, but how do I actually put them into practice? As a student, this question has also been a focus of mine, so let me take a few moments to highlight my strategy (as suggested by Cal Newport) and the reason for each step. You will see lots of the strategies that Cal discusses, as well as some improvements that I have made.

The Student Samurai

Soon, you too could be knocking tasks out like your favourite cartoon character. Let me show you exactly how:

  • First, we need to know what we need to do, and by when. Use your favourite to do list, be it digital or paper and pen, to do this.
  • We then need to condense this information into a calendar, so you can plan to do certain tasks in certain sections of the day
  • Finally, make sure trivial tasks such as taking the trash out, minor cleaning and planning meetups are clumped together and done in one part of the day. This way we can focus and get these done as fast as we can.

Reason

The reason that we create a huge todo list first is to give our brain the freedom to list out all its desires. Then, by prioritising this onto a calendar we are setting a clear goal for ourselves which we will strive to achieve. This is much better than, say, just checking things off as you do them. Finally, clumping minor tasks together allows Deep Work to take place, so that we can truely focus on the main tasks in the main parts of the day.

Pitfalls

Some major problems with this method are that predicting timing of each task can be a challenge. I normally address this with a technique called Kaizen, which definitely deserves its own blogpost. However, in short, Kaizen is the practice of repeatedly enforcing small positive changes based on the outcome from a previous decision, which in this case would be whether you went under or over time, and you would base future predictions on that task accordingly. There‚Äôs a lot more to Kaizen, and I really suggest reading ‚ÄúOne Small Step Can Change Your Life‚Äù to find out more about this technique.

3 Quick Ways to reclaim your time

In his book ‚ÄúDeep Work‚Äù, Cal Newport discusses the importance of focus to achieve the task at hand. He discusses that companies, rather than embodying deep work principles, are now discouraging it with new open offices and Instant Messaging. Whether or not you agree with his principles, we can all agree that a rejection of some alternatives here would lower our stress and increase productivity. Here are some of my techniques for doing just that:

  • Reduce Stimulus: How many times in the last hour has your phone vibrated due to some notification? Turning my phone onto constant Do Not Disturb mode (not even vibrations) has significantly increased my focus. I no longer get distracted by IM notifications. Remember to whitelist apps such as your calendar which help you keep track of time.

  • Set a goal to work Monday to Friday 9 to 5: Recall that when we discussed motivation, we set up small actions and committed to doing just those? It‚Äôs tough, I get it. Been there done that. Something is always delayed and takes up the whole weekend. But this is just one of the ‚Äúdo something‚Äù actions that you can adopt that will create further inspiration for yourself.

  • Work ‚Äòin a flow state‚Äô: Most people work ‚Äòbusy and anxious‚Äô. This is because they are constantly reacting to stimulus. When you are in a flow state, you will be indifferent to stimulus, since you will be constantly achieving your targets.
